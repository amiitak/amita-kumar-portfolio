Problem

Generic plans don't fit real people.

Most nutrition and fitness apps ask you what you ate. They log calories, track macros, and give you a plan based on a height/weight formula. The result is generic: a 1,800 calorie plan that ignores your sleep schedule, stress levels, dietary restrictions, and actual body composition goals.

The insight behind NutriCoach is that personalization has to start with the whole person, not just their food diary. By combining a photo (for body composition context) with goals and lifestyle inputs, we can generate plans that are actually tailored to where someone is starting from and where they want to go.

Discovery

What existing apps get wrong.

I audited the top nutrition apps (MyFitnessPal, Noom, Lose It, Calibrate) and ran user interviews across three fitness archetypes: beginners trying to lose weight, intermediate users trying to build muscle, and active people trying to optimize performance. Three patterns emerged consistently.

📊
Calorie counting fatigue
Users drop off when logging feels like a second job. Manual food entry is the number one reason for churn across all major apps.
🧍
One-size-fits-all plans
BMI-based recommendations ignore body composition, muscle mass, and lifestyle context. Users feel the plans aren't built for them.
🔗
Nutrition and fitness are siloed
Most apps do food OR fitness. Users have to stitch together multiple tools, losing the synergy between what they eat and how they train.
Strategy

The whole-person approach.

NutriCoach flips the input model. Instead of starting with food logs, we start with you: a photo for visual body composition context, your goals, your lifestyle constraints, and your dietary preferences. Claude Vision analyzes the photo alongside the structured inputs to generate a fully integrated plan covering meals, workouts, macros, and a grocery list.

The core product bet is that reducing input friction while increasing output relevance drives retention. If your plan actually fits your life, you stick to it.

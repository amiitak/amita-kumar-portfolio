When you open Yelp or Google Maps looking for a restaurant, you get a list sorted by proximity and stars. The algorithm knows your location. It knows aggregate ratings. What it does not know: you are taking your boss to dinner and need somewhere that feels impressive without being stuffy. You are on a third date and want somewhere with good energy but quiet enough to talk. You want to spend under $60 but not have it feel cheap.

These are context signals that drive real dining decisions, and no existing product captures them. TableMind is built on one premise: the right recommendation requires understanding the person, not just the place.

Discovery

How people actually choose restaurants.

I ran user research with LA diners, asking them to walk me through their last few restaurant decisions. Four patterns emerged consistently that no current product addresses.

🎭
Occasion drives everything
The number one input in every decision was the occasion: first date, work dinner, birthday, casual hangout. People described this before they mentioned cuisine, neighborhood, or budget.
🔇
Vibe is a real requirement
"Good for conversation," "not too loud," "feels special without being pretentious." Everyone mentioned vibes as a filter. Stars don't capture this. Reviews partially do, but you have to read 200 of them.
😩
Choice overload causes paralysis
People don't want 50 options. They want 3 good ones with a clear reason why each fits their specific situation. The value is in the curation and explanation, not the breadth.
📱
Influencer culture distorts recommendations
The most-photographed restaurants in LA are often optimized for content, not food. The best-kept local spots rarely have a PR strategy. Yelp and Google surface what is popular, not what is actually good. TableMind weights local knowledge over social signal, which means the algorithm can surface the unpretentious taco spot that beats the Instagrammed brunch place every time.
AI Product Design

The recommendation engine design.

TableMind is not a search wrapper. It uses a weighted multi-attribute scoring algorithm running over a curated dataset of LA restaurants. Claude scores each restaurant against your specific inputs across 5 dimensions, then generates a ranked list with transparent tradeoff reasoning. Here is how the algorithm works.

Dimension Weight What it measures How it's scored
Occasion fit 30% How well the restaurant matches the occasion type Tagged per restaurant: romantic, business, casual, celebration, date
Vibe match 25% Noise level, energy, atmosphere alignment Attributes: quiet/lively, intimate/social, trendy/classic
Location 20% Neighborhood match and proximity context Neighborhood tags + cross-neighborhood penalty
Budget fit 15% Price per person alignment with stated budget Price tiers: under $30, $30-60, $60-100, $100+
Cuisine match 10% Cuisine type alignment with preference Primary and secondary cuisine tags per restaurant
Why curated data beats Yelp for this use case
The Yelp API gives you ratings, categories, and price level. It does not tell you whether a restaurant is good for a first date, whether it's too loud for a business dinner, or whether locals actually go there. The curated dataset trades breadth for depth: 75 carefully selected LA restaurants with 12 attributes each, including vibe tags and honest caveats that no review algorithm surfaces.
Claude as the reasoning layer, not the database
The structured data does the scoring. Claude's job is to interpret the scores in the context of your specific inputs, generate natural-language explanations for why each restaurant fits, surface the honest tradeoff for each pick, and ask a clarifying follow-up question when the brief is ambiguous. This separation of concerns is deliberate: the algorithm ensures consistency, Claude ensures the output feels human.
Evals: how we know the recommendations are good
I evaluate TableMind across three dimensions. Relevance: does the top pick actually fit the stated occasion and vibe? Tradeoff accuracy: is the stated tradeoff real and honest, not a hedge? Explanation quality: would a local agree with the reasoning? In production, this would be measured via explicit user ratings after each recommendation session, feeding a continuous improvement loop on the dataset attributes and scoring weights.
Strategy & Product Plan

Built for LA first.

The strategy is hyper-local before it's scalable. A recommendation engine is only as good as its data, and generic data produces generic recommendations. Starting with a deeply curated LA dataset covering Silver Lake, Los Feliz, WeHo, Venice, DTLA, Brentwood, Culver City, and more ensures the recommendations feel like they came from a local who knows the city, not an algorithm that scanned the internet.

  • Phase 1 (current): 75-restaurant LA dataset covering 8 neighborhoods. Weighted scoring algorithm. Claude reasoning layer. Working prototype.
  • Phase 2: User feedback loop — after each recommendation session, users rate whether they went and whether they loved it. Feedback reweights the scoring algorithm over time. This is where it becomes a real recommendation engine.
  • Phase 3: Expand to NYC, SF, Chicago with the same curation model. License the dataset-plus-algorithm as a white-label product for hotel concierge apps and travel platforms.
  • North star metric: % of recommendations that result in a reservation within 24 hours.
  • Business model: B2C freemium with premium for unlimited searches and saved history, plus B2B licensing to hospitality platforms.
